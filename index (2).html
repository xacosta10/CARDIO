Introduction

The Information Cycle

When you find a source, it helps to understand where it falls in the information cycle. A social media post published right after an event is very different from a peer-reviewed journal article published a year later. Different assignments may call for different types of sources, so knowing how information develops over time will help you make better choices.

The Information Cycle: how information about an event develops over time, from social media posts to scholarly research.

You will encounter the term peer-reviewed throughout this tutorial. Peer-reviewed sources are scholarly articles that have been evaluated by other experts in the field before being published. They are generally considered among the most credible types of sources for academic research. The diagram below shows how that process works.

What is CARDIO?

When you are writing a research paper or completing an assignment, not all sources are equally trustworthy. The CARDIO method is a tool to help you evaluate whether a source is appropriate and credible to use in your work.

Each letter stands for a different criterion to consider when looking at a source. In this tutorial, you will learn what each letter means, see real examples, and answer a question to check your understanding before moving on.

The practice source used throughout this tutorial is a blog article titled The Effects of Caffeine Consumption Among College Students.

One of the most important skills in evaluating sources is lateral reading. Instead of reading deeply into a single source to decide if it is trustworthy, you open new browser tabs and search for what others say about the author or organization. Professional fact-checkers use this technique because it is faster and more reliable than trying to evaluate a source on its own. Watch the short video below to see lateral reading in action.

C

Currency

Is the information up to date?

Currency refers to the timeliness of the information, when it was published, last updated, or last reviewed. Not every topic requires the newest sources, but for fast-moving fields like medicine, technology, or current events, outdated information can be misleading or inaccurate.

When checking currency, ask yourself:

  • Is the information up to date?
  • How important is currency to your topic?
Important: Currency matters relative to your topic. A source about the history of the Civil War from 1995 may still be valid, but a source about current medical treatments could be critically outdated within just a few years.

A

Authority

Who created this information, and are they qualified?

Authority is about evaluating the credibility of the source's creator, whether that is an individual author, an organization, or a publisher. Anyone can post information online, so authority helps you determine whether the person or group behind a source actually knows what they are talking about.

When checking authority, ask yourself:

  • Is the author qualified to speak knowledgeably about your topic?
  • What are their credentials?
  • What can you learn about the author by reading laterally?
  • Does the source tell you if they are affiliated with a reputable institution or organization?
Lateral Reading: Instead of only reading the source itself, open new browser tabs and search for the author or organization in other sources. This is one of the most effective fact-checking techniques used by professional researchers.

R

Relevance

Does this source actually help answer your research question?

Relevance asks whether a source is actually useful for your specific assignment or research question. A source can be credible, current, and well-documented, but if it does not address your topic, it does not belong in your paper.

When checking relevance, ask yourself:

  • Does the source help answer your research question?
  • Is the information too advanced to understand or too simple for your assignment?
  • What information in this source directly relates to or supports your topic?
Assignment Check: Always re-read your assignment prompt before evaluating relevance. A source might be related to your broad topic but still not directly answer your specific research question.

D

Documentation

Is the information supported by quality evidence?

Documentation refers to how well an author supports their claims, through citations, references, data, or other evidence. Good documentation shows the author did their research and allows you to trace their sources to verify accuracy.

When checking documentation, ask yourself:

  • How in-depth is the information you have found?
  • Is the information supported by quality evidence?
  • Note if and how the author documents or cites their sources.
Citation Chains: A well-documented source gives you a built-in reading list. You can follow an author's citations to find additional credible sources on your topic.

I

Information Type

What kind of source is this, and is it appropriate?

Information Type means identifying what kind of source you are looking at, a news article, blog post, scholarly journal article, book, government report, and so on, and evaluating whether that type is appropriate for your academic assignment.

When checking information type, ask yourself:

  • What type of information is it (e.g., news article, journal article, book)?
  • Should you use this type of source in your assignment?
  • Is this type of source appropriate to use as evidence for this assignment?
Know Your Assignment: If your professor requires peer-reviewed or scholarly sources, a blog post, even a well-written one from a credible author, typically does not qualify. Always check your assignment instructions first.
The Information Cycle: different types of sources become available at different points after an event. Understanding this helps you evaluate a source's type and purpose.

O

Objectivity

Is the information fair, balanced, and free from bias?

Objectivity asks you to consider how biased or opinion-driven a source is, and whether it presents information fairly. Bias does not automatically disqualify a source, but you need to recognize it and account for it in how you use the information.

When checking objectivity, ask yourself:

  • How objective is the author?
  • Is the information fact based (supported by their documentation) or an opinion?
  • Does the source avoid emotional or biased language when discussing different viewpoints?
  • Do the author(s) or sponsor(s) make their purpose clear (to sell, to entertain, etc.)?
Bias Exists Everywhere: Even reputable sources have perspectives. The key is to recognize bias rather than pretend it does not exist. Using a variety of sources with different viewpoints strengthens your research.
Types of media bias to be aware of when evaluating sources for objectivity.

Media bias can appear in many forms, from the way a story is framed to what information is left out entirely. Understanding the difference between reader bias, like confirmation bias, and source bias, like corporate or political bias, helps you evaluate whether a source is presenting information fairly or pushing a particular agenda.
