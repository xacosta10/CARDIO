C

Currency

Is the information up to date?

Currency refers to the timeliness of the information — when it was published, last updated, or last reviewed. Not every topic requires brand-new sources, but for fast-moving fields like medicine, technology, or current events, outdated information can be misleading or just plain wrong.

When checking currency, ask yourself:

  • When was the source published or last updated?
  • Is the publication date clearly listed?
  • How important is recency to your research topic?
  • If links or references are included, are they still working and current?
💡 Pro Tip: A source about the history of the Civil War from 1995 may still be perfectly valid — but a source about COVID-19 treatment options from 2020 might be critically outdated. Currency matters relative to your topic.

Authority

Who created this information — and are they qualified?

Authority is about evaluating the credibility of the source's creator — whether that's an individual author, an organization, or a publisher. Anyone can post information online; authority helps you determine whether the person or group behind the source actually knows what they're talking about.

When checking authority, ask yourself:

  • Who is the author? What are their credentials or expertise?
  • Is the author affiliated with a reputable institution or organization?
  • What can you learn about the author by reading laterally (searching for them in other sources)?
  • Does the publisher or sponsoring organization have a reputation for accuracy and reliability?
🔍 Lateral Reading: Instead of just reading the source itself, open new browser tabs and search for the author or organization in other sources. This is one of the most powerful fact-checking techniques used by professional researchers.

R

Relevance

Does this source actually help answer your research question?

Relevance asks whether a source is actually useful for your specific assignment or research question. A source can be credible, current, and well-documented — but if it doesn't address your topic, it doesn't belong in your paper.

When checking relevance, ask yourself:

  • Does this source directly address my research topic or question?
  • Is the level of the information appropriate — not too advanced or too simplistic?
  • What specific information in this source supports my argument or thesis?
  • Would my instructor consider this type of source appropriate for this assignment?
✏️ Assignment Check: Always re-read your assignment prompt before evaluating relevance. A source about college student sleep habits might be tangentially related to your paper on caffeine — but does it actually help you answer your specific question?

D

Documentation

Is the information supported by quality evidence?

Documentation refers to how well an author supports their claims — whether through citations, references, data, links, or other evidence. Good documentation shows the author did their research and allows you to trace their sources to verify their claims.

When checking documentation, ask yourself:

  • Does the author cite their sources (references, footnotes, bibliography)?
  • Are the claims supported by data, research, or expert opinion?
  • Can you follow up on the sources they reference to verify accuracy?
  • Is the depth of coverage appropriate for the type of source it is?
📚 Citation Chains: One powerful research technique is following a source's citations to find additional credible sources on your topic. A well-documented source gives you a built-in reading list!

I

Information Type

What kind of source is this — and is it appropriate for your assignment?

Information Type means identifying what kind of source you are looking at — a news article, blog post, journal article, book, government report, advertisement, etc. — and evaluating whether that type is appropriate for your academic assignment.

When checking information type, ask yourself:

  • What type of source is this? (e.g., scholarly article, news article, blog, social media post)
  • Does this type of source meet the requirements of your assignment?
  • Is this a primary, secondary, or tertiary source?
  • Does the publication venue (journal, newspaper, website, etc.) affect its credibility for your purpose?
🎓 Know Your Assignment: If your professor requires "peer-reviewed" or "scholarly" sources, a blog post — even a well-written one from a credible author — typically doesn't qualify. Always check your assignment instructions first!

O

Objectivity

Is the information fair, balanced, and free from bias?

Objectivity asks you to consider how biased or opinion-driven a source is, and whether it presents information fairly. Bias doesn't automatically disqualify a source, but you need to recognize it and account for it in how you use the information.

When checking objectivity, ask yourself:

  • Does the author present multiple perspectives, or only one point of view?
  • Is the language emotionally charged or sensationalized?
  • Is the information fact-based and supported by documentation, or primarily opinion?
  • Does the author or sponsoring organization have a clear agenda (to sell, persuade, or entertain)?
⚖️ Bias Exists Everywhere: Even reputable sources have perspectives. The key is to recognize the bias rather than pretend it doesn't exist. Using a variety of sources with different viewpoints strengthens your research.
